Fractional Statistics In Two Dimensions

Introduction

The division of particles into two mutually exclusive classes - Bosons and Fermions - is a familiar one. In quantum mechanics, the distinction is made according to the nature of the state vector describing two indistinguishable particles of a given species: two identical fermions will always have an antisymmetric state vector; two identical bosons a symmetric one. Equivalently, under the exchange of two identical particles, a fermionic state vector picks up a phase factor of \(\exp(i\pi)\) (a minus sign), whereas the same action involving bosons leaves the corresponding state vector unchanged.

More generally, the identity of a particle is associated with a phase \(\displaystyle\lambda\Delta\theta\) acquired following the rotation of two identical particles around each other by an angle \(\Delta \theta\). Here \(\lambda\) is identified as the statistics and has a value of \(1\) and \(0\) for fermions and bosons, respectively (Box 1). Together with conventional interactions, this parameter governs the behaviour of collections of identical particles.

The spin-statistics theorem, proven in three dimensions, relates the magnitude of spin of a particle - an invariant - to its statistics. Particles with a spin \(s\) equal to an odd multiple of \(1/2\) are fermions, while those with a spin to equal to an even multiple of \(1/2\) are bosons. Succinctly: \(\lambda = 2s \ (\text{mod }2)\). Moreover, in three dimensions, these are the only possibilities .

In two dimensions, however, this is not the whole story. In fact, in \(d=2\) there are not only fermions and bosons, but particles with any statistics in between. Such particles are known as anyons and are the subject of this poster. The prototypical anyonic system - the 'cyon' - is examined below. In particular, the fractional nature of its spin (The Cyon System) and statistics (The Aharonov-Bohm Effect). Two dimensional systems are prevalent in condensed matter physics, hence the potential for anyons to be realised is great. Here we consider the relevance of anyons to the fractional hall effect (The Fractional Quantum Hall Effect).

If one particle is in a state described by the label \(a\), and the second in a state described by \(b\), the statistics \(\lambda\) determines the phase factor \(\exp(i\lambda \pi)\) associated with the exchange \(a \leftrightarrow b\). For fermions, setting \(b=a\) gives \(|aa\rangle=-|aa\rangle\), which implies that \(|aa\rangle=0\); two identical fermions cannot be in the same quantum state (Pauli exclusion Principle). Bosons suffer no such restriction.

The Cyon System

The cyon consists of a non-relativistic particle of charge \(q\) and mass \(m\) orbiting a magnetic flux tube of strength \(\Phi\), localised at the origin and perpendicular to the orbital motion (\(xy\)-plane). Such a tube may be realised by a thin magnetic solenoid of infinite extent along the \(z\) axis, with associated field \(\boldsymbol{B}=\Phi \delta(x)\delta(y)\bf{\hat{z}}\).

Neglecting any free motion along the axis of the solenoid, the system is effectively two-dimensional and found to exhibit fractional statistics. In this section we establish that the mechanical angular momentum of the cyon is shifted from the expected integer values by a fractional amount. This surprising result was firstly recognised by Frank Wilczek (Figure 1) in his 1982 seminal paper .

Denoting the position of the particle by \(\boldsymbol{r}\), the governing Lagrangian is

\[ \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}m\boldsymbol{\dot{r}}^2+ \frac{q}{c}\boldsymbol{\dot{r}}\cdot\boldsymbol{A} \]

Here \(\boldsymbol{A}(\boldsymbol{r})\) is a vector potential for \(\boldsymbol{B}\); \(\nabla \wedge \boldsymbol{A}=\boldsymbol{B}\). The direction and axial symmetry of \(\boldsymbol{B}\) mean that \(\boldsymbol{A}=A_{\varphi}(\rho)\boldsymbol{\hat{\varphi}}\), where \(\boldsymbol{\hat{\varphi}}\) is a azimuthal unit vector for the cylindrical coordinates \([\rho,\varphi,z]\).

To obtain the associated Hamiltonian we need the canonical momentum

\[ \boldsymbol{p}_c = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\dot{r}}} = m\boldsymbol{\dot{r}}+ \frac{q}{c}\boldsymbol{A}, \]
which differs from the familiar mechanical momentum \(m\boldsymbol{\dot{r}}\) by virtue of \(\boldsymbol{A}\). Then

\[ \mathcal{H}(\boldsymbol{r},\boldsymbol{p}_c) =\boldsymbol{p}_c\cdot\boldsymbol{\dot{r}}-\mathcal{L}=\frac{1}{2}m\boldsymbol{\dot{r}}^2 = \frac{1}{2m}\left(\boldsymbol{p}_c-\frac{q}{c}\boldsymbol{A}\right)^2 \]

Moving to a quantum setting, the relevant Schrödinger equation is, in the coordinate representation,

\[ \hat{H} \psi(\boldsymbol{r}) = \frac{1}{2m}\left(i\hbar \nabla + \frac{q}{c}\boldsymbol{A}\right)^2 \psi(\boldsymbol{r}) = E \psi(\boldsymbol{r}) \]

This may be solved using separation of variables, aided by the fact that \(\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{A}=0\) for our solenoid configuration. Of interest is that the angular part of the solution has the expected form \(\exp(im\varphi)\), with \(m \in \mathbb{Z}\) to attain a single-valued wavefunction.

Next we appeal to the rotational invariance of \(\mathcal{H}\) about the \(z\)-axis: the canonical orbital angular momentum along this axis, \(\ell_c=\left[\boldsymbol{r}\wedge \boldsymbol{p}_c\right]_z\), is a constant of motion. With \(\boldsymbol{p}_c\) as above,

\[ \ell_c=\left[\boldsymbol{r}\wedge m\boldsymbol{\dot{r}} + \boldsymbol{r}\wedge \frac{q}{c}\boldsymbol{A}\right]_z = \ell_z + \frac{q\Phi}{2\pi c} \]

where \(\ell_z\) is the mechanical angular momentum.

Now, the canonical angular momentum is always associated with the operator \(\displaystyle -i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial \varphi}\), and so is quantised as \(\hbar m\) when acting upon the angular dependence \(\exp(im\varphi)\) of our solution .

It follows that the mechanical angular momentum has the spectrum

\[ \ell_z = \hbar \left(m - \frac{q\Phi}{hc}\right), \qquad m \in \mathbb{Z} \]

In a subtle twist, it is this angular momentum, and not \(\ell_c\), that is the relevant quantity when describing phenomenon local to the cyon; Goldhaber and Mackenzie provided a complete argument for this several years after Wilczek's original paper . As a result, it is appropriate to identify the intrinsic angular momentum or spin of the cyon as the value of \( \ell_z\) when \(m=0\) (units \(\hbar\)):

\[ s = \left.\frac{\ell_z }{\hbar}\right\rvert_{m=0} = -\frac{q\Phi}{hc} \tag{\(\dagger\)}\label{cyon_spin} \]
We see that, in general, \(s\) is neither integer nor half-integer.

The Aharonov-Bohm Effect

In 1959, Yakir Aharonov and David Bohm devised a series of experiments to demonstrate that the potentials \(\boldsymbol{A}\) and \(\phi\) had independent significance in quantum theory (Figure 2) .

In particular, the pair found that the wave function of a non-relativistic charged particle moving around a closed path \(\Gamma\) in space-time acquires a phase

\[ -2\pi i\frac{q}{h}\oint_\Gamma\left( dt \phi- d\boldsymbol{r} \cdot\frac{\boldsymbol{A}}{c} \right) \tag{\(\ddagger\)} \]

From this result it is a short step to the fractional statistics of the charged particle-flux system described in the previous section: Consider a full revolution in space (\(dt=0\)) of the charge around the flux-tube, which has localised field \(\boldsymbol{B}=\Phi \delta(x)\delta(y) \boldsymbol{\hat{z}}\). Using Stokes' theorem, expression \((\ddagger)\) gives

\[ -2\pi i\frac{q}{h c}\oint_\Gamma d\boldsymbol{r} \cdot \boldsymbol{A}=-2\pi i\frac{q}{hc}\iint dxdy\, B = -2\pi i \frac{q \Phi}{h c} \]

For a two-cyon state vector \(|1,2\rangle\), exchange generates twice this phase as the particle of each cyon is moved around the flux tube of the other. Continuing the notation used in the introduction, \(\Delta \theta = 2\pi\) and the statistics of the cyon is \[ \lambda= - 2\frac{q\Phi}{hc} \]

Not only is \(\lambda\) fractional, but comparison of its value to that obtained for the spin of the cyon \((\dagger)\) indicates that the conventional spin-statistics relation holds in this case too; that is, \(\lambda=2s\).

The Fractional Quantum Hall Effect

The Quantum Hall Effect (QHE) occurs in two-dimensional electronic systems subject to very low temperatures and strong magnetic fields. It is characterised by the quantisation of the transverse Hall conductance \(\sigma_H\) in units of \(e^2/h\): \[ \sigma_H = \nu \frac{e^2}{h} \] Here \(\nu\), known as the filling factor, takes integer or certain fractional values in the integer and fractional QH effects, respectively. The corresponding Hall resistance shows a number of plateaux labelled by \(\nu\) (Figure 3). For an explanation of the origin of quantum Hall effects, see the recent exposition by Z. Ezawa .

In the theory of the fractional QHE, the usual electron gas condenses into a peculiar collective ground state resembling an incompressible liquid [3]. In the state \(\nu=1/m\) with \(m\in \mathbb{Z}\) odd, excitations take the form of quasielectron-quasihole pairs [9], where each quasiparticle posses a fractional charge \(\pm e^*\), with \[ e^* = e/m \]

Such a fractional property is reminiscent of anyons, and this is not misleading. For, in 1984, Daniel Arovas, John Schrieffer and Frank Wilczek demonstrated that these quasiparticles also obeyed fractional statistics , and hence are indeed anyons.

The study of systems exhibiting the fraction QHE continues to be a very active research area, with several unanswered questions. One such question is why a fractional state is observed at \(\nu=5/2\), since the standard theory predicts fractional states at \(\nu = n/m\) for odd \(m\) only. Here anyons with an exotic form of exchange statistics, known as non-abelain anyons, may well provide an answer . It is clear that fractional statistics will prove an integral part of any complete theory of the fractional QHE.

According to the classical theory of an electron gas, the Hall resistance \(R_H=1/\sigma_H\) is linear in the applied magnetic field \(B_\bot\). While this accurately describes experimental results for weak magnetic fields (\(\lesssim 0.3\ T\)) [9], in stronger fields \(R_H\) shows a stair case as the Hall conductance undergoes transitions between quantised values labelled by \(\nu\) (note that \(\nu^{-1} \propto B_\bot\) according to the theory).
